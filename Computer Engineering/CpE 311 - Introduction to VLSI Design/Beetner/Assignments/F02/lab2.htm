CpE 311 Lab Exercise 2

VHDL Based FPGA Design

Due: Tue, Oct. 8.

Purpose

The purpose of this exercise is to become familiar with VHDL based design flow for Actel Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGA).  In this lab you will design a medium scale circuit using VHDL and logic synthesis in order to form a basis for comparison to schematic based design. You will use the same specifications as you used in Lab 1.  

Problem Description

Repeat lab 1 but use VHDL to capture your design instead of a schematic.  Synthesize your design using Exemplar's Leonardo synthesis tool.

Implement your registered ALU with an Actel 40MX02 FPGA. Use the same pin assignment for the PLC44 package that you used in lab 1. Perform a functional and timing simulation. Use the Model Technology VHDL compiler and simulator, Exemplar's Leonardo Spectrum synthesis tool, and Actel Designer place and route.  Extract a VHDL post route netlist and an SDF timing file in order to perform a timing simulation of your circuit.

You may use vsim's stimulus language for your own testing of your circuit.  Your design must also successfully pass tests provided to you in the form of a VHDL test bench.  The testbench will be provided.  Your report should include the list output from the testbench showing all of the entity port signals and any other internal signals you think are relevant.

Do NOT simply convert your lab 1 schematic design to a VHDL structural netlist.  Organize your model in the form of separate combinational logic and sequential logic processes.  Create an equivalent entity for each “symbol” you used in your design in lab1 and then wire up the entities accordingly.  Use the following entity for your top-level design (the registered alu):

library ieee;
use ieee.std_logic_1164.all;
use ieee.numeric_std.all;
entity ralu is
 port(B: in std_logic_vector(3 downto 0); -- external operand
      S: in std_logic_vector(2 downto 0); -- function code
      RST: in std_logic;                  -- reset (active high)
      Sysclk: in std_logic;               -- system clock
      ACC: out std_logic_vector(3 downto 0); -- accumulator out
      CY: out std_logic);                 -- carry out bit
end entity ralu;

 

If the entity is not specified in the above manner, then the testbench will not work.

Report

Your report should include as a minimum the following items:

  • Title: CpE311 Lab Exercise 2 - Actel ALU with Accumulator design
  • Your name, and date
  • VHDL source code.
  • Simulation list output (not timing diagrams) for both functional and timing simulation.  Compare the timing simulation output to the static timing analysis output and explain any differences.
  • The number of Actel logic modules used in your design
  • Worst case propagation delay between registers. Correlate the timing analysis output with your VHDL source code.
  • Maximum clock rate for your design and a brief discussion of how it was calculated.
  • The approximate amount of time you spent on the exercise separated into design, design capture, and testing phases.
  • Compare the size and timing metrics obtained in lab 2 to those obtained in lab 1.
  • A short narrative explaining your design and test rationale.

Do not use any form of binder or protector for your report.  Staple in the upper left corner only.  Your report should not exceed 10 pages maximum.  Include all primary source data (stuff you type) but do not include any computer generated output other than the simulation list output for your functional and timing simulations.

 

Hints

 

·      See the “Act1 Synthesis Example” for further help.

·      Testbench.  Be sure to analyze your .vhd file with vcom before you analyze the testbench.  Since the testbench will instantiate your alu design, your design should already exist.  The testbench will take about 850 ns to run and will announce “test completed” when it is done.

·      Use DT analyze to find the maximum delay between registers and the critical path – Not Spectrum.  To get to dtanalyze, type designer at the unix prompt, load your design as in lab 1, compile it, lay it out (hit the Layout button), then hit DT Analyze.  Choose your desired parameters and hit OK (be sure to specify a source and sink – start and finish point).  The table shows you the delays in descending order.  If you select one and hit “Expand”, it will give you details about the delay path.

·      If you’d like to “see” what your analyzed circuit looks like, you may use Leonardo to do so.  As presented in the slides given in class, type “leonardo” at the command prompt, select the appropriate technology and your .vhd file and then press “run flow” (this may take a minute or two).  Under the “Tools” menu item, you may view the RTL or gate-level synthesized version of your VHDL code.

·      To run the testbench while using your timing parameters, use the command:

vsim –sdfmax /dut=hdl/alu.sdf ralu_tb a1

where alu.syn should be replaced by the name of the sdf file created for your design. This command will specify that the sdf timing parameters are applied only to the entity instance named “dut” (which is your alu in this case) within the testbench entity.
